Both SDTM findings class and ADaM BDS class are used to present the results of a test. In SDTM, we present the result using --STRESC or --STRESN variables, while in ADaM we present the results using AVAL and AVALC variable. As per ADaM standard, the value used in PARAMCD for a test should uniquely qualify the result stored in AVAL or AVALC (that is, no additional qualifiers are needed to explain the test). Whereas in SDTM, we can have additional qualifier variables to the --TESTCD variables to understand the test performed.
Example 1:
If systolic blood pressure is collected in sitting and standing positions for a subject, we use the value of "SYSBP" in VSTESTCD and use VSPOS qualifier to indicate "SITTING" or "STANDING" position of the subject. Here, VSPOS is qualifying the VSTESTCD variable for the result stored in VSSTRESN or VSSTRESC. As we cannot have any additional qualifiers for PARAMCD variable value, we combine the information of qualifier variables and TESTCD value to create a unique PARAMCD value. In this example, a paramcd value of SISBP can be used to indicate the sitting systolic blood pressure and STSBP can be used to indicate the standing systolic blood pressure.

Understanding where a clinical programmer fits in the drug development pipeline is a common interview topic. Here is a clear breakdown:

A clinical SAS programmer (also called a statistical programmer or clinical data programmer) is responsible for converting raw clinical trial data into analysis-ready datasets and regulatory submission deliverables. Their work sits between data management and biostatistics.

Core responsibilities include:

  • Programming SDTM datasets from raw (CDASH) data following the SDTM Implementation Guide and study-specific specifications
  • Programming ADaM datasets (ADSL, ADAE, ADLB, ADTTE, etc.) per the SAP and ADaM Implementation Guide
  • Producing Tables, Figures, and Listings (TFLs) as specified in the shells document and SAP
  • Writing and maintaining validation programs (double programming / QC) to ensure accuracy
  • Creating submission deliverables: define.xml, reviewer's guides (cSDRG, ADRG), and dataset-level documentation
  • Collaborating with biostatisticians, data managers, and medical writers throughout the study lifecycle

To summarise: the clinical programmer is the bridge between raw collected data and the clean, structured, submission-ready evidence packages that regulators review. Every number in a study report traces back to programmer code — making accuracy, traceability, and standard compliance critical skills.

Interviewers often ask this to gauge whether a candidate understands the practical rhythm of the role — not just the technical concepts. Here is what a typical day looks like:

Routine programming activities:

  • Writing or updating SDTM and ADaM programs in response to data updates, protocol amendments, or specification changes
  • Running and reviewing TFL outputs after data cuts or data management milestones
  • Performing QC (quality control) — comparing independently written programs to identify discrepancies
  • Reviewing and resolving validation flags raised by tools like Pinnacle 21

Collaborative activities:

  • Attending kick-off and status meetings with biostatisticians, data managers, and project managers
  • Reviewing or contributing to programming specifications (SDTM specs, ADaM specs, TFL shells)
  • Raising and resolving queries with data management regarding data quality or inconsistencies
  • Supporting medical writers during the CSR (Clinical Study Report) phase by confirming numbers and providing annotated outputs

Submission activities (study close-out):

  • Finalising define.xml and reviewer's guides
  • Packaging and validating study data transport files for regulatory submission

Key takeaway: No two days are identical. The balance shifts from heavy programming early in a study to heavy QC and documentation near a data lock or submission. Flexibility and strong communication skills matter as much as SAS proficiency.

The CRF is one of the most fundamental documents in clinical data collection — interviewers expect you to know it well and to understand how it connects to the programmer's work.

A Case Report Form (CRF) is the printed or electronic document used by investigators at clinical sites to record all required information about each trial subject. It is designed by the sponsor or CRO to capture exactly the data specified in the protocol.

An eCRF (electronic Case Report Form) is the electronic equivalent — data is entered directly into an EDC (Electronic Data Capture) system rather than on paper. Most modern trials use eCRFs.

Why the CRF matters to a programmer:

  • The blank CRF shows all collected fields — essential for understanding what raw data is available
  • The DM-annotated CRF maps each CRF field to its corresponding database variable name — used by data management
  • The SDTM-annotated CRF maps each CRF field to its target SDTM variable and dataset — the primary reference for SDTM programming

Remember: the programmer never sees the CRF in isolation — they work from the SDTM-annotated version (also called aCRF). Confirming the aCRF aligns with the SDTM spec is a critical first step before writing a single line of code.

EDC stands for Electronic Data Capture. It is the software platform used at clinical sites to enter, validate, and transmit patient data collected during a trial.

How it works:

  • Site staff (coordinators, investigators) enter data directly into the EDC system via eCRFs
  • The EDC applies real-time edit checks to flag obvious data entry errors (e.g., a lab value outside a plausible range)
  • Data managers review, query, and clean the data within the EDC
  • Once cleaned and locked, the EDC exports raw data (often in SAS transport or CSV format) to the sponsor/CRO for programming

Common EDC platforms used in the industry: Medidata Rave, Oracle Clinical/InForm, Veeva Vault EDC, OpenClinica.

Programmer's perspective: The programmer typically receives raw data exports from the EDC — these are the CDASH-structured (or study-specific) datasets that feed into SDTM programming. The programmer does not work inside the EDC directly, but understanding how data flows from EDC to SDTM is important for tracing data lineage and explaining derivations.

Key point: Data quality at the EDC stage directly impacts programming. A well-designed edit check strategy reduces the number of data issues programmers encounter downstream.

This is a high-value interview question. Being able to narrate the end-to-end data journey demonstrates genuine domain understanding. Here is the flow:

  1. Protocol & CRF design: The sponsor defines what data to collect based on the protocol. The CRF is designed to capture this data at sites.
  2. Data collection (EDC): Site staff enter subject data into the eCRF in the EDC system. Real-time edit checks validate data entry.
  3. Data management & cleaning: Data managers review data, issue queries to sites, and clean discrepancies. A Data Management Plan (DMP) governs this process.
  4. Raw data transfer: Cleaned data is exported from the EDC (typically as SAS datasets or CSV files) to the programming team.
  5. SDTM programming: Programmers map raw data to SDTM datasets per the SDTM IG and study-specific specs. An annotated CRF documents the mapping.
  6. ADaM programming: ADaM datasets (ADSL, ADAE, ADLB, etc.) are derived from SDTM per the SAP and ADaM IG.
  7. TFL production: Tables, Figures, and Listings are generated from ADaM datasets and reviewed against the SAP shells.
  8. CSR & submission package: The Clinical Study Report is finalized. Submission deliverables (SDTM + ADaM datasets, define.xml, reviewer's guides, TFLs) are assembled and submitted to the regulator (FDA, EMA, etc.).

Takeaway: The programmer is involved at steps 5–8. Understanding the full chain — especially steps 1–4 — helps programmers ask the right questions and interpret data correctly.

The SAP is one of the most important documents a clinical programmer works from. Knowing its purpose and contents is essential for any interview.

A Statistical Analysis Plan (SAP) is a formal document that describes in detail the statistical methods that will be used to analyse the trial data. It is written by the biostatistician, reviewed by the medical team, and finalized (often locked) before data lock to prevent post-hoc analysis decisions.

What a SAP typically contains:

  • Analysis populations (ITT, mITT, PP, Safety — with precise definitions)
  • Primary, secondary, and exploratory endpoints with their statistical methods
  • Handling of missing data, outliers, and protocol deviations
  • Subgroup analyses and multiplicity adjustments
  • TFL shells (mock tables, figures, and listings showing exact layout and content)
  • ADaM dataset derivation rules for analysis variables (AVAL, BASE, CHG, PCHG, flags)

Programmer's relationship with the SAP:

  • ADaM datasets are programmed to support every analysis defined in the SAP
  • TFL programs replicate the statistical outputs exactly as specified in the SAP shells
  • Any ambiguity in the SAP must be resolved by raising a query to the biostatistician — never assumed

Key point: The SAP is the contract between the statistician and the programmer. Deviating from it without documented approval is a compliance risk.

The CSR is the culmination of the entire clinical trial — it is worth understanding its structure and the programmer's role in producing it.

A Clinical Study Report (CSR) is a comprehensive document that describes the design, conduct, and results of a clinical trial. It is submitted to regulatory authorities as part of a marketing approval application. The structure is governed by ICH E3 guidelines.

Key sections of a CSR (ICH E3 structure):

  • Synopsis: brief summary of the trial
  • Introduction and objectives
  • Investigational plan (study design, protocol)
  • Study patients (disposition, demographics, protocol deviations)
  • Efficacy evaluation
  • Safety evaluation (adverse events, lab findings, vital signs)
  • Discussion and conclusions
  • Appendices: TFLs, patient narratives, protocol, SAP, define.xml

Programmer's contribution to the CSR:

  • All TFLs in the CSR appendices are programmer-generated outputs
  • In-text numbers (counts, percentages, p-values) must match TFL outputs exactly — programmers are often asked to verify in-text numbers against their programs
  • The annotated CRF (aCRF) and define.xml are also programmer deliverables included in the CSR appendices

Remember: The CSR is a regulatory document. Any numerical error traced back to a programming mistake has direct implications for drug approval and patient safety.
