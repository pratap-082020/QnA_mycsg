Distinguishing between AE and SAE is essential knowledge for any clinical programmer — AE data forms one of the most important datasets (ADAE) and is a key focus of safety reviews.

Adverse Event (AE): Any untoward medical occurrence in a subject administered a pharmaceutical product. An AE does not need to have a causal relationship with the treatment. Examples: headache, nausea, dizziness.

Serious Adverse Event (SAE): An AE that meets at least one of the following seriousness criteria (as per ICH E6):

  • Results in death
  • Is life-threatening
  • Requires inpatient hospitalisation or prolongation of existing hospitalisation
  • Results in persistent or significant disability or incapacity
  • Is a congenital anomaly or birth defect
  • Is a medically important event that may jeopardise the subject or require medical intervention to prevent one of the above outcomes

Common confusion — Severity vs. Seriousness:

  • Severity (AESEV) describes the intensity of an event: mild, moderate, or severe
  • Seriousness (AESER) is a regulatory category based on the criteria above
  • A severe AE is not automatically a serious AE. Example: a severe headache is severe in intensity but may not meet any SAE criterion
  • Conversely, an SAE can be mild in severity — e.g., a mild congenital abnormality in an offspring

SDTM mapping: In the AE domain, AESER = 'Y' or 'N' captures seriousness; AESEV captures severity. AESMIE captures "medically important event" criterion.

These terms are closely related but carry different weight. Interviewers ask this because protocol deviations impact analysis populations — particularly the Per Protocol population.

Protocol Deviation: Any departure from the procedures set out in the approved protocol. Deviations can be minor (unlikely to affect subject safety or data integrity) or major (potentially affecting subject safety, data integrity, or the reliability of results).

Protocol Violation: Used interchangeably with major protocol deviation by many organisations. A violation is a departure serious enough to warrant removal of the subject from the Per Protocol (PP) analysis population. Some companies use the term "violation" specifically for the most egregious deviations that would not be acceptable in a regulatory submission.

Examples:

  • Minor deviation: a visit conducted 3 days outside the protocol-specified window
  • Major deviation/violation: administration of a prohibited concomitant medication; subject was not eligible per inclusion/exclusion criteria but was randomised anyway

Impact on analysis populations:

  • Subjects with major deviations/violations are typically excluded from the Per Protocol (PP) population
  • They remain in the ITT and Safety populations
  • In SDTM, deviations are captured in the DV (Protocol Deviations) domain
  • In ADaM ADSL, flags such as PPROTFL (Per Protocol Flag) are derived based on the absence of major deviations

Screen failures are a routine part of clinical trials and affect subject disposition data — a common programming area.

A screen failure occurs when a subject provides informed consent and undergoes screening assessments, but is found to not meet one or more eligibility criteria (inclusion/exclusion criteria) defined in the protocol and therefore does not proceed to randomisation.

Key points:

  • Screen failures are enrolled (consented) but not randomised — they appear in the subject disposition but not in any treatment arm
  • Screen failure rates can be substantial (30–50% in some disease areas) and affect study timelines and site performance
  • Data collected during screening from screen failures is typically not included in efficacy or safety analyses, though the disposition data itself is always reported

SDTM representation:

  • Screen failures appear in the DS (Disposition) domain with DSTERM/DSDECOD indicating the reason for screen failure
  • In ADSL, screen failures may be included with a specific flag or excluded entirely, depending on the SAP — always check the study-specific definition
  • The disposition table (commonly the first table in a CSR) shows counts of screened subjects, screen failures, and randomised subjects

Analysis populations are a core interview topic for clinical programmers because they directly drive the derivation of ADSL population flags.

Intent to Treat (ITT) population: Includes all randomised subjects, regardless of whether they received the treatment, complied with the protocol, or completed the study. The ITT analysis preserves the benefits of randomisation and minimises bias. It is the most common primary analysis population in confirmatory trials.

Modified Intent to Treat (mITT) population: A subset of the ITT population. The exact definition varies by study, but commonly includes all randomised subjects who received at least one dose of study treatment. The word "modified" signals that some ITT subjects have been excluded based on a pre-specified criterion — always check the SAP for the study-specific mITT definition.

Per Protocol (PP) population: A subset of the ITT population that includes only subjects who completed the trial without major protocol deviations and who were sufficiently compliant with treatment. The PP analysis can provide a best-case estimate of treatment effect but is susceptible to selection bias.

Safety population: Typically all subjects who received at least one dose of study treatment. Used for all safety analyses.

ADSL flag derivation:

  • ITTFL — Intent to Treat Flag (Y/N)
  • MITTFL — Modified ITT Flag (Y/N), study-specific definition
  • PPROTFL — Per Protocol Flag (Y/N), excludes major deviations
  • SAFFL — Safety Flag (Y/N), at least one dose received

Tip: In any study you work on, confirm population definitions from the SAP before deriving flags — definitions differ across studies and sponsors.

The washout period is a critical design element in crossover studies — understanding it demonstrates awareness of how study design affects data.

A washout period is a deliberate interval between two treatment periods in a crossover study, during which no study treatment is administered. Its purpose is to allow the effects of the first treatment (and the drug itself) to fully dissipate from the subject's body before the next treatment begins.

Why it matters:

  • Without an adequate washout, the effect of Treatment A may still be present when the subject starts Treatment B — this is called a carryover effect
  • Carryover bias can make Treatment B appear more (or less) effective than it actually is, invalidating the within-subject comparison
  • Washout duration is typically set at 5 half-lives of the drug — enough time for plasma concentration to fall below measurable levels

SDTM representation:

  • The washout period is captured as an EPOCH in SDTM (e.g., EPOCH = "WASHOUT PERIOD 1", "WASHOUT PERIOD 2")
  • Assessments during the washout period should be attributed to the correct epoch in domain datasets

ADaM implication: Washout period observations are generally excluded from analysis unless the SAP specifies otherwise. Period-level variables in ADaM must correctly reflect which treatment period an observation belongs to.

Run-in periods appear in many study designs, particularly in cardiovascular, respiratory, and CNS trials. Knowing what they are and their effect on data is important.

A run-in period is a phase at the beginning of a clinical trial, before randomisation, during which all subjects receive the same treatment (or placebo) under controlled conditions. The run-in period serves one or more of the following purposes:

  • Placebo run-in (single-blind): All subjects receive placebo before randomisation. Used to identify and exclude placebo responders and non-compliant subjects, ensuring a more homogeneous population enters the randomised phase
  • Active treatment run-in: All subjects receive the active drug before randomisation. Used in studies where all randomised subjects must be stable on a background therapy (e.g., all subjects placed on a standard-of-care drug before being randomised to add-on therapy)
  • Washout run-in: Subjects discontinue prior medications — essentially a washout before the randomised phase begins

SDTM representation: The run-in period is captured as an EPOCH (e.g., EPOCH = "RUN-IN"). Data collected during run-in is mapped to appropriate SDTM domains with the correct epoch attribution.

ADaM implication: Run-in assessments are commonly used to derive baseline values. The SAP will specify whether baseline is the last assessment before randomisation (which may fall within the run-in period) or before first dose.

Understanding endpoint hierarchy is fundamental — endpoints directly drive the structure of the SAP and the TFLs that programmers produce.

Primary endpoint: The main outcome measure that the trial is powered to detect. It is the basis for the primary efficacy analysis and for the regulatory decision on approval. There is typically one primary endpoint per trial (or a small number in composite designs). The sample size calculation is based on the primary endpoint.

  • Examples: Overall Survival (OS) in oncology; HbA1c change from baseline in diabetes; ADAS-Cog score change in Alzheimer's

Secondary endpoints: Additional outcomes that support and contextualise the primary finding. They provide important information about other aspects of treatment benefit and safety. Secondary endpoints are pre-specified in the SAP and reported in the CSR.

  • Examples: Progression-Free Survival (PFS), quality of life scores, time to first response
  • Note: multiplicity adjustment may be required for secondary endpoints in confirmatory trials to control the overall Type I error rate

Exploratory endpoints: Hypothesis-generating outcomes analysed without pre-specified statistical testing plans. Results are descriptive and cannot be used to make confirmatory claims. They inform future studies.

  • Examples: biomarker responses, subgroup analyses, novel scoring systems not yet validated

Programming note: ADaM datasets must support all three levels of endpoints. The SAP will list each endpoint and its corresponding ADaM parameter(s). TFLs for primary endpoints receive the most intense QC scrutiny.

Informed consent is a regulatory and ethical requirement — it is the mechanism by which a subject voluntarily agrees to participate in a trial after being fully informed of its nature, risks, and alternatives.

Key elements of informed consent:

  • Written, signed, and dated consent from the subject (or legally acceptable representative) before any study procedures are performed
  • The consent form must explain: purpose of the trial, procedures involved, foreseeable risks and benefits, alternatives to participation, confidentiality arrangements, and the subject's right to withdraw at any time
  • Obtaining informed consent is the investigator's responsibility. It must be documented in the subject's records.

SDTM representation: Informed consent date is typically captured in the DS (Disposition) domain (DSTERM = "INFORMED CONSENT OBTAINED", DSDECOD = "INFORMED CONSENT OBTAINED") and/or in the DM (Demographics) domain via RFICDTC (Date/Time of Informed Consent).

Why it matters: No study procedures — including screening assessments — can be performed before informed consent is obtained. Any data collected before consent is a protocol deviation. Programmers should be aware that RFICDTC is a key reference date variable used in deriving study day variables.
