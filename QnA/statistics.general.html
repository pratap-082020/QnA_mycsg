Randomization is the process of assigning subjects to treatment groups by chance to minimize selection bias and balance known and unknown confounders.

Common methods:
  • Simple randomization: Like flipping a coin for each subject. Easy to implement but can lead to imbalanced group sizes, especially in small trials.
  • Block randomization: Subjects are randomized within blocks of a fixed size (e.g., blocks of 4: AABB, ABAB, ABBA, etc.). Ensures approximately equal group sizes throughout the trial. Block size is kept confidential to prevent prediction.
  • Stratified randomization: Randomization is performed separately within strata defined by important prognostic factors (e.g., site, disease severity, age group). Ensures balance of key covariates across treatment groups.
  • Stratified block randomization: Combines both — block randomization within each stratum. This is the most common method in Phase III trials.
  • Dynamic/adaptive randomization (minimization): Uses an algorithm that considers the current balance of prognostic factors when assigning each new subject. Achieves better balance than stratified randomization when there are many factors, but is more complex.
In SAS programming: Randomization schedules are typically generated by the biostatistician using PROC PLAN or specialized software. The programmer may need to merge the randomization list with treatment assignments during unblinding.
Blinding (masking) is the practice of keeping treatment assignments hidden to reduce bias in assessment and reporting of outcomes.

Types of blinding:
  • Open-label (unblinded): Both the subject and the investigator know the treatment assignment. Used when blinding is impractical (e.g., surgical vs medical intervention). Higher risk of assessment bias.
  • Single-blind: The subject does not know which treatment they receive, but the investigator does. Reduces placebo effect but the investigator's knowledge can still bias assessments.
  • Double-blind: Neither the subject nor the investigator knows the treatment assignment. This is the gold standard for most Phase III trials. Requires matching placebos (same appearance, taste, packaging).
  • Triple-blind: Subject, investigator, and the data analyst/statistician are all blinded. The statistician works with coded treatment groups (e.g., "Treatment A" vs "Treatment B") until the final analysis is unblinded.
Maintaining the blind:
  • Matching placebos must be identical in appearance.
  • Lab results or side effects that could reveal treatment (e.g., known drug side effects) must be managed carefully.
  • Emergency unblinding procedures must exist for safety.
For SAS programmers: During blinded analysis, you program against dummy treatment labels. At database lock and unblinding, the actual randomization codes are merged in.
Sample size calculation determines the minimum number of subjects needed to detect a clinically meaningful treatment difference with adequate statistical power.

Key inputs:
  • Significance level (α): Typically 0.05 (two-sided).
  • Power (1 - β): Typically 80% or 90%.
  • Effect size: The minimum clinically meaningful difference the trial should detect. Based on prior studies, literature, or clinical judgement.
  • Variability (SD): Expected standard deviation of the primary endpoint. Estimated from prior data.
  • Dropout rate: Expected proportion of subjects who will not complete the study. The calculated n is inflated to account for this.
For common designs:
  • Two-sample t-test: n per group = 2 × [(z_α/2 + z_β)² × σ²] / δ², where δ is the treatment difference.
  • Chi-square (proportions): Based on expected proportions in each group.
  • Time-to-event: Based on expected number of events, hazard ratio, and enrollment/follow-up duration.
In practice: Biostatisticians perform these calculations using tools like nQuery, PASS, or SAS PROC POWER. The sample size justification is included in the protocol and SAP. An underpowered study risks missing a real effect; an overpowered study wastes resources and exposes unnecessary subjects to experimental treatment.
A superiority trial aims to demonstrate that the experimental treatment is better than the control (placebo or active comparator) by a statistically significant margin.

Hypotheses:
  • H₀: μ_treatment - μ_control = 0 (no difference)
  • H₁: μ_treatment - μ_control ≠ 0 (there is a difference)
Success criterion: The p-value for the treatment comparison must be < α (typically 0.05), and the confidence interval for the treatment difference must exclude zero.

Key points:
  • This is the most common trial design for demonstrating efficacy of a new drug vs placebo.
  • Both the statistical significance AND the clinical meaningfulness of the difference should be considered.
  • A statistically significant but clinically trivial difference may not support regulatory approval.
Example: A trial comparing Drug A to placebo for reducing HbA1c. If the mean difference is -0.8% with 95% CI (-1.1%, -0.5%) and p < 0.001, the trial successfully demonstrates superiority — Drug A reduces HbA1c significantly more than placebo.
A non-inferiority (NI) trial aims to demonstrate that the experimental treatment is not unacceptably worse than an active comparator (reference treatment). It does not need to be better — just "not too much worse."

Why use non-inferiority?
  • When a placebo arm is unethical (effective treatments already exist).
  • When the new treatment has other advantages: fewer side effects, easier administration, lower cost.
Key concept — the non-inferiority margin (Δ or M):
  • A pre-specified threshold that defines the maximum acceptable loss of efficacy.
  • Must be clinically justified and smaller than the known effect of the active comparator vs placebo.
  • Choosing Δ requires regulatory input and is one of the most debated aspects of NI trial design.
Hypotheses (for a "higher is better" outcome):
  • H₀: μ_new - μ_reference ≤ -Δ (new treatment is inferior by more than the margin)
  • H₁: μ_new - μ_reference > -Δ (new treatment is not inferior)
Success criterion: The lower bound of the 95% CI for the treatment difference must be above -Δ.

Important distinctions from superiority:
  • NI trials use an active comparator, not placebo.
  • Both ITT and per-protocol populations are important (ITT can be anti-conservative in NI trials).
  • Assay sensitivity must be established — the active comparator must have proven efficacy.
An equivalence trial aims to demonstrate that two treatments have similar efficacy — that any difference between them is clinically negligible in both directions.

Hypotheses:
  • H₀: |μ_new - μ_reference| ≥ Δ (the treatments differ by more than the margin)
  • H₁: -Δ < μ_new - μ_reference < Δ (the treatments are equivalent within the margin)
Success criterion: The entire 95% CI for the treatment difference must fall within (-Δ, +Δ).

How it differs from non-inferiority:
  • Non-inferiority only tests one direction (not worse than).
  • Equivalence tests both directions — neither worse nor better by more than Δ.
  • Equivalence uses two one-sided tests (TOST procedure) at α/2 each, or equivalently checks that the entire CI is within the margin.
Common use cases:
  • Generic drug approval (bioequivalence studies) — demonstrating that a generic has the same bioavailability as the branded drug.
  • Biosimilar studies.
  • Formulation comparison studies.
Bioequivalence example: For pharmacokinetic parameters (AUC, Cmax), the standard equivalence margin is 80%–125% on the ratio scale (using log-transformed data). The 90% CI of the geometric mean ratio must fall entirely within this range.
Clinical trials define multiple analysis populations, each serving a different purpose:

Intent-to-Treat (ITT):
  • Includes all randomized subjects, regardless of whether they received treatment, completed the study, or adhered to the protocol.
  • Preserves the benefits of randomization.
  • Considered the most conservative and unbiased estimate of treatment effect in superiority trials.
  • This is the primary analysis population for most superiority trials per ICH E9.
Modified Intent-to-Treat (mITT):
  • A subset of ITT that excludes subjects with specific post-randomization issues — e.g., subjects who never received any study drug, or who had no post-baseline efficacy assessment.
  • Exclusion criteria must be pre-specified in the SAP.
  • Often used as the primary population in practice when strict ITT is impractical.
Per-Protocol (PP):
  • Includes only subjects who completed the study without major protocol deviations — adhered to the treatment, had no prohibited medications, etc.
  • Represents the "ideal" scenario but is subject to selection bias.
  • Usually a supportive/sensitivity analysis in superiority trials.
  • In non-inferiority trials, PP is equally important because ITT tends to shrink differences toward zero, which is anti-conservative for NI.
Safety Population: All subjects who received at least one dose of study medication — used for all safety analyses.
Interim analysis is a planned evaluation of accumulating data at one or more time points before the study is complete. It allows early decisions about efficacy, futility, or safety.

Why interim analyses are done:
  • Early efficacy: Stop the trial early if the treatment effect is overwhelmingly positive — ethical to make effective treatment available sooner.
  • Futility: Stop early if there's little chance of demonstrating efficacy — avoids wasting resources and unnecessary exposure of subjects.
  • Safety: Monitor for unexpected safety signals via a DSMB (Data Safety Monitoring Board).
The multiplicity problem:
  • Each look at the data is an opportunity to falsely declare significance (inflated Type I error).
  • If you test at α = 0.05 at each of 3 interim analyses plus the final, the overall Type I error exceeds 0.05.
Alpha spending functions control the overall Type I error by allocating portions of α to each analysis:
  • O'Brien-Fleming: Very conservative at early looks (very small α spent), more liberal at the final analysis. The most commonly used approach. Example: At 2 interim + 1 final analysis, boundaries might be p < 0.0005, p < 0.014, p < 0.045.
  • Pocock: Spends α more evenly across looks. Equal boundaries at each analysis but requires a smaller final α.
  • Lan-DeMets: A flexible spending function approach — α can be spent at any information fraction without pre-specifying the exact number of looks.
In practice: Interim analyses are conducted by an independent DSMB/DMC. The sponsor's team typically remains blinded. SAS programmers may prepare DSMB reports under special access controls.

Multiplicity arises when multiple statistical tests are performed in the same study (multiple endpoints, multiple treatment comparisons, subgroup analyses). Each test carries a risk of Type I error, and without adjustment, the overall (family-wise) error rate inflates beyond α = 0.05.

Common adjustment methods:
  • Bonferroni correction: Divide α by the number of tests (k). Test each at α/k. Simple but very conservative — works well for a small number of comparisons.
  • Holm (step-down Bonferroni): Order p-values from smallest to largest. Test the smallest at α/k, next at α/(k-1), etc. Less conservative than Bonferroni while still controlling family-wise error.
  • Hochberg (step-up): Similar to Holm but starts from the largest p-value. Slightly more powerful but requires independence of tests.
  • Fixed-sequence (hierarchical) testing: Endpoints are tested in a pre-specified order. If the first is significant at α, proceed to the next at α, and so on. Testing stops at the first non-significant result. Common in Phase III trials with primary and key secondary endpoints.
  • Gatekeeping procedures: A generalized version of hierarchical testing that handles families of endpoints (e.g., primary family must pass before testing secondary family).
  • Dunnett's test: For comparing multiple treatment groups to a single control group.
  • Tukey's HSD: For all pairwise comparisons in ANOVA.
In clinical trials: The multiplicity strategy is pre-specified in the SAP and is a key topic in regulatory discussions. The strategy balances controlling Type I error with maintaining power to detect real effects.
Endpoints are the measured outcomes used to evaluate treatment effects. They are organized in a hierarchy:

Primary endpoint:
  • The most important outcome that the trial is designed to evaluate.
  • Sample size calculation is based on this endpoint.
  • Regulatory approval decisions are primarily based on the primary endpoint result.
  • Example: Change from baseline in HbA1c at Week 24 for a diabetes drug.
  • Usually one (sometimes two co-primary) — having co-primary endpoints requires both to be significant, which reduces power.
Secondary endpoints:
  • Additional outcomes that support or supplement the primary endpoint.
  • Tested in a pre-specified hierarchical order to control multiplicity.
  • Example: Proportion of patients achieving HbA1c < 7%, change in fasting plasma glucose, change in body weight.
  • A positive secondary result strengthens the overall evidence for the drug.
Exploratory (tertiary) endpoints:
  • Hypothesis-generating analyses — not used for confirmatory conclusions.
  • No multiplicity adjustment applied.
  • Results are reported with nominal p-values and described as exploratory.
  • Example: Biomarker analyses, quality of life scores, post-hoc subgroup analyses.
Key principle: Only the primary endpoint (and sometimes key secondary endpoints tested within a multiplicity framework) provide confirmatory evidence. Everything else is supportive or hypothesis-generating.
Safety analysis evaluates the adverse effect profile of the treatment. It is a critical component of every clinical trial submission.

Key considerations:
  • Analysis population: The Safety population (all subjects who received at least one dose) is used, not ITT.
  • Treatment assignment: Subjects are analysed according to the treatment they actually received (as-treated), not what they were randomized to.
  • No multiplicity adjustment: Safety analyses are typically descriptive — no formal hypothesis testing or multiplicity correction. The goal is to identify signals, not confirm hypotheses.
Standard safety analyses include:
  • Adverse Events (AEs): Overall AE summary, AEs by System Organ Class (SOC) and Preferred Term (PT) using MedDRA coding. Frequency tables showing n (%) for each treatment group.
  • Serious Adverse Events (SAEs): Separate summary and patient listings.
  • Treatment-emergent AEs (TEAEs): AEs that started or worsened after the first dose of study drug.
  • AEs leading to discontinuation.
  • Deaths.
  • Laboratory data: Shift tables (normal/low/high at baseline vs post-baseline), potentially clinically significant (PCS) values, change from baseline summaries.
  • Vital signs: Summary statistics and PCS values.
  • ECG: QTc prolongation analysis (especially important for cardiac safety).
For SAS programmers: Safety tables and listings form the bulk of the TFL package. Treatment-emergent flags, MedDRA coding, and shift table derivations are key ADaM/SDTM programming tasks.
Missing data is inevitable in clinical trials (patient dropouts, missed visits, incomplete assessments). How missing data is handled can significantly impact the study conclusions.

Types of missing data (Rubin's classification):
  • MCAR (Missing Completely at Random): Missingness is unrelated to any observed or unobserved data. Rare in practice.
  • MAR (Missing at Random): Missingness depends on observed data but not on the unobserved values. Most methods assume MAR.
  • MNAR (Missing Not at Random): Missingness depends on the unobserved values themselves (e.g., patients drop out because they're getting worse). Most problematic — requires sensitivity analyses.
Common methods:
  • MMRM (Mixed Model for Repeated Measures): The preferred primary analysis method for continuous longitudinal endpoints (per ICH E9 R1). Assumes MAR. Uses all available data without explicit imputation. Implemented via PROC MIXED in SAS.
  • LOCF (Last Observation Carried Forward): Replaces missing values with the last observed value. Simple but biased — assumes no further change after dropout. Was once standard; now considered a sensitivity analysis only.
  • Multiple Imputation (MI): Creates multiple plausible datasets with imputed values, analyses each, then combines results using Rubin's rules. Accounts for imputation uncertainty. SAS: PROC MI → analysis → PROC MIANALYZE.
  • WOCF (Worst Observation Carried Forward): Uses the worst observed value. Very conservative — used as a sensitivity analysis.
  • Tipping point analysis: Systematically worsens imputed values for the treatment group to assess how much deviation from MAR is needed to overturn the primary result.
Best practice (ICH E9 R1): Pre-specify a primary estimand (what you're trying to estimate including how intercurrent events like dropout are handled), use MMRM or MI as the primary method, and include multiple sensitivity analyses under different missing data assumptions.
Subgroup analysis examines whether the treatment effect differs across pre-defined subsets of the study population.

Common subgroups:
  • Age (< 65 vs ≥ 65 years)
  • Sex (male vs female)
  • Race/ethnicity
  • Geographic region
  • Baseline disease severity
  • Prior therapy use
Important principles:
  • Subgroup analyses should be pre-specified in the SAP to be credible.
  • They are generally exploratory — not powered to detect subgroup effects.
  • The key test is the treatment-by-subgroup interaction, not the within-subgroup p-value. A significant interaction suggests the treatment effect truly differs across subgroups.
  • Multiple subgroup comparisons without adjustment increase the risk of false positives.
Forest plot:
  • The standard way to display subgroup results.
  • Shows the treatment effect estimate and 95% CI for each subgroup as horizontal lines with point estimates.
  • A vertical reference line at the null value (0 for differences, 1 for ratios).
  • The overall treatment effect is typically shown at the bottom.
  • Interaction p-values are displayed alongside.
Regulatory perspective: Regulators expect to see consistency of treatment effect across subgroups. Large differences may trigger questions about whether the drug works for the entire population or only specific subsets.
The type of primary endpoint determines the statistical method used for efficacy analysis:

Continuous endpoints (e.g., change from baseline in HbA1c, blood pressure, pain score):
  • Primary method: ANCOVA or MMRM, adjusting for baseline value and stratification factors.
  • Treatment effect: Difference in LS means between treatment groups.
  • SAS: PROC MIXED (MMRM) or PROC GLM (ANCOVA).
  • Reported as: LS mean difference, 95% CI, and p-value.
  • Example: "The LS mean difference in HbA1c change from baseline at Week 24 was -0.8% (95% CI: -1.1, -0.5; p < 0.001) favouring Drug A over placebo."
Binary endpoints (e.g., response rate, proportion achieving target):
  • Primary method: Cochran-Mantel-Haenszel (CMH) test stratified by randomization factors, or logistic regression.
  • Treatment effect: Difference in proportions, odds ratio, or relative risk.
  • SAS: PROC FREQ (CMH) or PROC LOGISTIC.
  • Reported as: Proportions per group, treatment difference with 95% CI, odds ratio with 95% CI, and p-value.
  • Example: "Response rate was 45% (Drug A) vs 28% (Placebo); OR = 2.1 (95% CI: 1.3, 3.4; p = 0.003)."
Time-to-event endpoints (e.g., overall survival, progression-free survival):
  • Primary method: Log-rank test, Cox proportional hazards model.
  • Treatment effect: Hazard ratio.
  • SAS: PROC LIFETEST (Kaplan-Meier, log-rank) and PROC PHREG (Cox model).
Statistical power in the context of a clinical trial is the probability that the study will detect a true treatment difference as statistically significant.

Formula: Power = 1 - β, where β is the probability of a Type II error (failing to reject H₀ when the treatment truly works).

Standard targets:
  • Most Phase III trials are designed with 80% or 90% power.
  • 80% power means there is a 20% chance of a false negative — the drug works but the trial fails to show it.
  • 90% power is used for pivotal trials where the cost of failure is very high.
What affects power in a clinical trial:
  • Sample size: The primary driver — more subjects = more power.
  • True treatment effect: Larger effects are easier to detect.
  • Variability: Higher SD in the endpoint reduces power (more noise).
  • Significance level (α): A stricter α (e.g., 0.01 vs 0.05) reduces power.
  • Dropout rate: More dropouts reduce the effective sample size.
  • Analysis method: Efficient methods (MMRM vs LOCF-ANCOVA) can improve power.
Consequences of inadequate power:
  • The trial may "fail" even though the drug works — wasting years and millions of dollars.
  • Regulators may not accept results from underpowered studies.
  • Additional studies may be required, delaying drug availability to patients.
For SAS programmers: While biostatisticians calculate sample size, understanding power helps you interpret results and appreciate why certain design choices (like stratification or MMRM analysis) are made — they improve precision and thus power.
